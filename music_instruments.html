01
Wind Family
Reed-Pipe Instruments
🎷
How Reed Instruments Work
Reed instruments produce sound when the player blows air past a thin piece of wood or cane called a reed. The air makes the reed vibrate extremely fast, creating sound waves. The tube (bore) around it shapes and amplifies these waves. By opening and closing holes along the tube, players change the length of the air column, producing different notes. Shorter air column = higher pitch. Longer air column = lower pitch.
🎷
Saxophone
Belgium, 1840s
Invented by Adolphe Sax in Brussels. Despite being made of brass, it's classified as a woodwind because it uses a single reed. Widely used in jazz, blues, and classical music. The reed is attached to the mouthpiece and vibrates when blown.
Single Reed
🎵
Clarinet
Germany, 1700s
Developed from earlier instruments called the chalumeau. Uses a single reed and cylindrical bore. One of the most versatile woodwind instruments, used in orchestras, jazz bands, and folk music around the world.
Single Reed
🎼
Oboe
France, 17th Century
One of the hardest woodwinds to master. The oboe uses a double reed — two pieces of cane bound together. When the player blows between them, both reeds vibrate against each other. Known for its bright, penetrating tone in orchestras.
Double Reed
🪗
Bassoon
Italy/Germany, 16th Century
The bass voice of the woodwind family. Uses a double reed like the oboe but is much larger — the tube is over 2.5 metres long, folded back on itself! Provides the deep, rich low notes in orchestras.
Double Reed
🎶
Shehnai (Shahnai)
India / South Asia
A traditional Indian reed instrument with deep cultural importance. Played at weddings, temples, and festivals. Uses a double reed. The legendary Ustad Bismillah Khan made it famous worldwide. It produces a piercing, soulful sound.
Double Reed
🪘
Bagpipes
Scotland / Middle East
Air is blown into a bag and squeezed out through pipes containing reeds. The bag acts as a reservoir, maintaining a constant airflow. The melody pipe (chanter) uses a double reed. Ancient forms existed in Persia, Egypt, and India.
Double Reed + Bag
02
Chordophone Family
Stringed Instruments
🎸
How Stringed Instruments Work
All stringed instruments produce sound by vibrating stretched strings. The strings are made to vibrate by plucking (guitar, harp), bowing (violin, cello), or striking (piano). The body of the instrument acts as a resonator — it amplifies the vibrations so we can hear them clearly. Thinner, shorter, or tighter strings vibrate faster → higher pitch.
🎻
Violin
Italy, 16th Century
The smallest and highest-pitched member of the bowed string family. The bow (horsehair on a wooden stick) is drawn across the strings causing them to vibrate. By pressing fingers on the neck, players shorten the string length to change pitch.
Bowed
🎵
Sitar
India, 13th Century
An iconic Indian classical instrument with a long neck and gourd resonator body. Has 18–21 strings (some are drone strings that aren't plucked). The curved frets allow players to bend notes beautifully — giving sitar its characteristic sound. Associated with Ravi Shankar globally.
Plucked
🎸
Guitar
Spain, 15th Century
One of the world's most popular instruments. Six strings stretched over a hollow wooden body. Plucking or strumming the strings causes them to vibrate; the hollow body amplifies the sound. Electric guitars use magnetic pickups to convert vibrations to electrical signals.
Plucked / Strummed
🪕
Sarod
India / Afghanistan
A classical Indian string instrument without frets — the player slides their fingernails on a metal fingerboard, allowing smooth, gliding tones. The body is covered in animal skin. It's known for its deep, resonant, meditative sound in Hindustani classical music.
Plucked
🎶
Harp
Ancient Mesopotamia / Egypt
One of the oldest instruments in the world — depictions date back to 3,000 BCE. Each string produces a different note, unlike most instruments where one string covers multiple notes. Pedals on modern harps can change tuning. Associated with angels and heavenly music in many cultures.
Plucked
🎼
Cello (Violoncello)
Italy, 16th Century
Held between the player's knees, the cello is the tenor/bass voice of the string family. Its four strings are much thicker than a violin's, producing lower frequencies. Widely considered one of the instruments closest in range and tone to the human voice.
Bowed
03
Keyboard Family
Keyboard Instruments
🎹
How Keyboard Instruments Work
Keyboard instruments trigger sound through a row of levers called keys. When a key is pressed, it sets off a mechanism that causes a string to vibrate (piano), a pipe to sound (organ), or an electronic circuit to activate (synthesizer). The keyboard layout is the same across most instruments — 88 keys on a grand piano, arranged in octaves of white and black keys.
🎹
Piano
Italy, 1700
Invented by Bartolomeo Cristofori in Florence. When a key is pressed, a padded hammer strikes a steel string. The name comes from "pianoforte" (soft-loud) — it was the first keyboard instrument that could play both softly and loudly. A grand piano has ~230 strings!
Struck Strings
⛪
Pipe Organ
Ancient Greece, ~200 BCE
The largest musical instrument! When a key is pressed, pressurised air is sent through pipes of different lengths. Longer pipes produce lower notes. Churches often have pipes taller than a person. Some organs have thousands of pipes. Known as the "King of Instruments."
Air Through Pipes
🎼
Harpsichord
Europe, 14th–15th Century
The predecessor of the piano. When a key is pressed, a small leather or quill plectrum plucks the string (unlike the piano which strikes it). This means the harpsichord can't vary its volume — all notes sound equally loud. Popular in Baroque music (Bach era).
Plucked Strings
🎛️
Synthesizer
USA, 1960s
Generates sound electronically — no physical strings or pipes. Electronic oscillators create sound waves that can be shaped, filtered, and modulated. Moog synthesizers (1965) revolutionized pop and rock music. Modern synths can imitate any instrument or create entirely new sounds.
Electronic
🪗
Accordion
Germany / Austria, 1820s
A hand-held reed instrument with a keyboard. Squeezing and expanding the bellows forces air over metal reeds (like small versions of a harmonica reed). The left hand plays bass notes and chords; the right hand plays the melody. Widely used in folk music across Europe and Latin America.
Bellows + Reeds
04
Membranophone & Idiophone
Percussion Instruments
🥁
How Percussion Instruments Work
Percussion instruments make sound when they are struck, shaken, or scraped. Some produce definite pitch (like xylophones and tablas), while others produce indefinite pitch (like cymbals and snare drums). Percussion is one of the oldest forms of music — humans were beating objects together before any other instrument existed.
🥁
Tabla
India, 18th Century
The most famous percussion instrument of Indian classical music. A pair of hand drums — the smaller dayan (right, higher pitch) and larger bayan (left, lower pitch). The black spot (syahi) on each drum head is a tuning paste that gives the tabla its unique melodic quality. Over 500 rhythmic patterns (taals) exist.
Struck by Hand
🎶
Mridangam
South India
The main percussion instrument in Carnatic (South Indian classical) music. A barrel-shaped, double-headed drum made from jackfruit wood. Like the tabla, it has paste applied to tune the drum heads. Played horizontally across the lap, using fingers and palms.
Struck by Hand
🔔
Xylophone
Africa / Southeast Asia
Wooden bars of different lengths are arranged like a keyboard. Striking them with mallets causes each bar to vibrate at a specific frequency. Longer bars produce lower notes. The name means "wood sound" in Greek. Used in orchestras and African folk music.
Struck Wooden Bars
🥁
Dhol
South Asia (India/Pakistan)
A large, barrel-shaped drum worn around the neck or hung from the shoulder. One side uses a heavy stick for the bass boom, while the other side uses a thin stick for a treble beat. Central to Bhangra music and Punjabi celebrations. Also used in Afghan and South Asian folk traditions.
Double-Headed Drum
🎵
Snare Drum
Medieval Europe
Has metal wires (snares) stretched across the bottom drum head. When the top is struck, the snares rattle against the bottom head, creating a sharp, crisp sound. The crisp "crack" of a snare drum is essential to virtually all Western popular music and marching bands.
Snare Wires + Struck
🔔
Cymbals
Turkey / Ancient Civilizations
Thin, circular metal discs that crash together or are struck with a drumstick. The alloy (usually bronze) vibrates all over its surface at multiple frequencies simultaneously — which is why cymbals have that "splashy," shimmering sound rather than a definite pitch. Zildjian (founded 1623) is the world's oldest cymbal maker.
Crashed / Struck Metal
05
Aerophone Family
Brass Instruments
🎺
How Brass Instruments Work
Despite the name, sound in brass instruments isn't made by the metal — it's made by the player's lips vibrating in a cup-shaped mouthpiece! The lips buzz together at high speed, and this buzzing vibration travels through the brass tubing. By pressing valves or moving a slide, players change the tube length to produce different pitches. Longer tube = lower pitch.
🎺
Trumpet
Ancient Egypt / Modern Europe
The highest-pitched brass instrument. Has three valves to change pitch. Ancient trumpets (without valves) date to 1,500 BCE in Egypt. The modern valve trumpet was developed around 1820. It's the lead voice of the brass section — bright, bold, and penetrating. Essential to jazz and orchestras.
3 Valves
🔊
French Horn
France / Germany, 17th Century
Has a conical bore that flares out into a wide bell. The player's right hand is inserted into the bell to modify tone. Uncoiled, the tubing would be over 3.7 metres long! Produces a warm, mellow sound compared to the trumpet's brightness.
Rotary Valves
🎷
Trombone
Europe, 15th Century
Unique among brass instruments — instead of valves, it uses a U-shaped slide that extends in and out to change pitch continuously. This allows smooth, gliding "glissando" effects. The trombone was one of the first instruments written into orchestral compositions.
Slide
📯
Tuba
Germany, 1835
The largest and lowest-pitched brass instrument. Invented by Wilhelm Wieprecht in Berlin. The enormous amount of tubing (up to 5.5 metres uncoiled) produces those deep, booming bass notes. It provides the foundation of the brass section in orchestras and marching bands.
Rotary / Piston Valves
🎵
Bugle
Europe, 18th Century
The simplest brass instrument — it has NO valves at all! Players change pitch only by adjusting the tension and speed of their lips. Limited to about 5 natural notes. Used historically by armies to signal commands (charge, retreat, reveille). The Last Post is played on a bugle.
No Valves (Lip Only)
🎺
Shehnai / Nadaswaram
South India
The Nadaswaram is a large, double-reed wind instrument from South India — technically a reed instrument, but often grouped with ceremonial winds like brass. It can reach over 90 decibels without amplification! Played at Hindu temple ceremonies and South Indian weddings.
Double Reed / Ceremonial

A Brief History
Instruments Through the Ages
~40,000 BCE — Stone Age
The oldest known musical instruments are flutes made from vulture bones and mammoth ivory, found in Germany. Drums and clappers (percussion) were likely the very first instruments used by early humans for ritual purposes.
~3,000 BCE — Ancient Civilizations
Harps and lyres were played in ancient Mesopotamia (present-day Iraq) and Egypt. Flutes, trumpets, and cymbals appeared in China and the Mediterranean. Music was used in temples, royal courts, and ceremonies.
~500 BCE — Classical Antiquity
Ancient Greeks developed music theory. The hydraulis (water organ) was invented, the ancestor of all pipe organs. Indian texts like the Natya Shastra catalogued instruments into four groups: strings, wind, membranes, and solid (a system still used today!).
~8th–15th Century CE — Medieval Era
Lutes arrived in Europe from Arabic-Islamic culture. The violin family began developing in Italy. Church organs became grander. In India, the veena, sitar, and tabla traditions were formalized. The sitar is thought to have been developed by Amir Khusrau.
16th–18th Century — Baroque & Classical Period
The violin reached its current form through Italian makers like Stradivari and Guarneri. The harpsichord was king of keyboard instruments. Bartolomeo Cristofori invented the piano (~1700). Brass valves were introduced, allowing more notes. Bach and Mozart composed for these instruments.
19th Century — Industrial Revolution
Mass production made instruments widely available. The saxophone was patented in 1846. The modern brass valve system was perfected. The concert grand piano reached its current form. Orchestras grew larger and louder to fill new concert halls.
20th–21st Century — Electronic Age
Electric guitars transformed popular music (1930s–50s). Moog synthesizers launched electronic music (1964). Digital sampling allows any sound to be triggered from a keyboard. Today, apps on a phone can simulate an entire orchestra, and new hybrid instruments combine ancient design with modern electronics.
🎵 World of Musical Instruments

Every culture on Earth has developed its own musical instruments — over 1,000 different types have been documented worldwide.

Music is a universal human language that connects us across time, geography, and culture.
